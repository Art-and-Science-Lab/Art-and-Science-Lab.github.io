Are Kilogram and Ampere (Coulomb) Fundamental Units?

Physics · Unit systems · Natural units

In the current International System of Units (SI), the kilogram and the ampere are defined as base units. However, this classification is an institutional convention established for practical measurement convenience and does not represent a necessity of natural science. In this work, we argue that mass and charge are not fundamental quantities, but constructed quantities derived from more primitive spatiotemporal relations. Furthermore, we show that the gravitational constant \(G\), the vacuum permittivity \(\varepsilon_0\), and the vacuum permeability \(\mu_0\) are proportionality constants introduced to preserve the internal consistency of the artificial unit system based on the kilogram and the coulomb, and are not intrinsic fundamental constants of nature (see References).
Historically, the unit of mass was first defined, followed independently by the unit of electric charge. Subsequently, the gravitational constant \(G\), the vacuum permittivity \(\varepsilon_0\), and the vacuum permeability \(\mu_0\) were introduced to ensure consistency among physical equations. However, reexamining Newtonian gravitational potential energy and Coulomb potential energy makes it clear that the kilogram and the coulomb are essentially human-defined units rather than natural necessities.
At the beginning of A Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism (1873), Maxwell noted that “if mass were defined in an astronomical system of units by its gravitational strength, then the dimensional formula of mass \(M\) would be \(L^3/T^2\).”

Current gravitational potential energy

\[ E = \frac{G\,m\,M}{r} \]

If the gravitational constant \(G\) is absorbed into the unit of mass (kilogram), the unit of mass is transformed into the dimension \(m^3/s^2\). In this work, signs are omitted since only dimensional relations among scalar quantities are considered.

Gravitational potential energy with \(G\) absorbed

\[ E' = \frac{m' M'}{r}, \qquad E' = E\cdot G, \qquad m' = m\cdot G, \qquad M' = M\cdot G \]

Since gravitational potential energy itself contains the kilogram in its numerator, the same absorption of \(G\) applies. A quantity possessing the same dimensional structure is Coulomb potential energy.

Current Coulomb potential energy

\[ E = \frac{k\,q\,Q}{r} = \frac{qQ}{4\pi \varepsilon_0 r} = \frac{qQ\,c^2\,\mu_0}{4\pi r} \]

Analogously, we consider absorbing artificial constants into the unit of charge (coulomb). However, since the speed of light \(c\) is a universal constant, it is not absorbed. Moreover, since vacuum permeability contains the kilogram in its dimension, the gravitational constant \(G\) is absorbed simultaneously.

Coulomb potential energy with artificial constants absorbed

\[ E = \frac{q' Q'\,c^2}{r}, \qquad q' = q\sqrt{\frac{\mu_0 G}{4\pi}}, \qquad Q' = Q\sqrt{\frac{\mu_0 G}{4\pi}} \]

If the speed of light were also absorbed into the coulomb, the expression would reduce to the Gaussian-unit form. However, retaining \(c\) is essential here. At this stage, the unit of charge becomes \(m^2/s\), and both the kilogram and the coulomb disappear, reduced to derived units composed solely of space and time.

From this, a direct relation between the elementary charge and the electron mass is obtained.

\[ m_e' = e'\,c\,\beta \]

Here, \(\beta\) is a dimensionless constant equal to the square root of the ratio between gravitational and Coulomb potential energies.
Furthermore, since the conversion factor from coulomb to \(m^2/s\) contains the square root of the kilogram, an additional simplification becomes possible. By absorbing \(\beta^2\) into the kilogram from the outset, the least precise constant, the gravitational constant \(G\), is eliminated from both mass and charge conversion factors.
The mass converter \(\mu\) and charge converter \(\varepsilon\) are then defined as follows, yielding the new-unit electron mass \(m_0\) and elementary charge \(e_0\).

\[ m_0 = \mu\,m_e, \qquad \mu = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\left(\frac{e\,c}{m_e}\right)^2 \]

\[ e_0 = \varepsilon\,e, \qquad \varepsilon = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\left(\frac{e\,c}{m_e}\right) \]

We refer to this new unit system as the Space-Time (ST) Unit system. In the ST system, physical structures that are hidden in the SI framework become directly visible. In particular, the ST system yields an extremely simple relation between the elementary charge and the electron mass, reminiscent of Einstein’s mass–energy equivalence.

\[ m_0 = e_0\,c \]

We have long accepted the kilogram and the coulomb as fundamental units without serious question. However, by reexamining artificial units and reconstructing the unit system at the electronic level, previously unseen aspects of physical law emerge with remarkable clarity.
